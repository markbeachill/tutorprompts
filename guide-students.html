Student guide

Where does the toolkit fit into your study workflow?

Use the toolkit after you have made your own attempt, then turn feedback into learning, revision and honest AI-use records.

Core rule

Bring the toolkit something you have tried.

The toolkit works best when you have already made an attempt: a sentence, paragraph, section, plan, proposal, feedback note or rough draft. It can help you understand what is not working and decide what to do next.

What the toolkit is for

  • Getting feedback on your own writing.
  • Understanding repeated mistakes.
  • Checking paragraph and whole-draft structure.
  • Testing whether an argument is clear.
  • Turning feedback into a revision plan.
  • Practising a skill before revising your own work.

What it is not for

Do not use the toolkit to get an AI tool to write your assignment for you, invent evidence, disguise AI authorship, or bypass your course rules. You remain responsible for the final wording, claims, evidence and submission.

A simple student workflow

  1. Try first. Draft the sentence, paragraph, section or plan yourself.
  2. Choose one library. Use a mini library if you want focused help, or the master library if you need the full toolkit.
  3. Type prompt. Ask the AI to show the menu and choose the tool that fits your problem.
  4. Paste only what is needed. Use a short extract unless your course rules and privacy situation allow more.
  5. Revise yourself. Use the feedback to make your own changes rather than copying a finished version.
  6. Record AI use if needed. Use SW3 if your course asks for an AI-use statement or you want a transparent record.

What not to paste or upload

Be careful with anything private or about other people. Do not paste names, student numbers, email addresses, interview transcripts, placement notes, client details, case studies, unpublished research, marks, feedback about another student, or confidential material unless you have checked your course, research ethics or institution rules.
