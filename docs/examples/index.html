Writing Tutor Library

Writing Tutor Library example

Student input

The implementation of influencer marketing has had an impact on the development of brand engagement among young consumers.

Sample output extract

The sentence makes sense, but it is heavy. The main action is hidden inside nouns such as “implementation” and “development”. A clearer sentence would show who is doing what. Your revision task is to name the actor and the action more directly.

What the student gets out of it

The tool does not just polish the sentence. It explains the pattern: hidden action, heavy nouns and unclear actors.

Structure Tutor Library

Structure Tutor Library example

In this example, the student describes the structure of their draft rather than pasting the full text. Some structure tools can work from a summary, although a real draft usually gives better evidence.

Student input

Paragraph 1 gives background on the causes of the English Civil War. Paragraph 2 jumps to Charles I’s personality. Paragraph 3 discusses Parliament. Paragraph 4 concludes that religion was the main cause.

Sample output extract

The structure has a possible argument, but the reader may not yet see how each paragraph builds towards the conclusion. If religion is the main cause, the earlier paragraphs need to prepare that claim rather than appearing as separate background points.

What the student gets out of it

The tool comments on sequence, central claim and reader orientation rather than rewriting the essay.

Academic Thinking Tutor Library

Academic Thinking Tutor Library example

Student input

Hand hygiene training clearly solves infection-control problems in hospitals.

Sample output extract

A critical reader may challenge “clearly solves”. The claim assumes a direct and complete cause-and-effect relationship. You may need to distinguish between training, actual practice, workplace constraints and infection outcomes.

What the student gets out of it

The tool pressures the argument. It asks what is assumed, what evidence is needed and how the claim could be made more careful.

Research Proposal Tutor Library

Research Proposal Tutor Library example

Student input

I want to study whether homework affects children’s learning and use interviews, surveys and classroom observation.

Sample output extract

The topic is too broad for a small research proposal. A supervisor would ask: which age group, which subject, what kind of homework, what kind of learning, and what data can you realistically collect?

What the student gets out of it

The tool acts like a critical supervisor. It checks focus and feasibility before the student spends time polishing sentences.

Study Workflow Tutor Library

Study Workflow Tutor Library example

Student input

My tutor said my business report has useful research, but the recommendations are too general and I need to link them more clearly to the evidence.

Sample output extract

Start with the recommendations, not the wording. Priority 1: identify which evidence supports each recommendation. Priority 2: make each recommendation more specific. Priority 3: check whether the report explains why the recommendation follows from the evidence.

What the student gets out of it

The tool helps the student decide what to do first instead of trying to fix everything at once.

Extended example

A short dialogue example

This placeholder example shows the toolkit as a conversation, not just a one-shot critique.

Student input

This paragraph is about how remote working changed teamwork. It says people communicate less and this has a bad effect on workers.

Toolkit response

The central claim is still vague. “Communicate less” could mean fewer meetings, weaker informal contact, slower decision-making or less trust. Before adding evidence, choose the specific relationship you want the paragraph to explain.

Your turn: Try one sentence that names the specific change in communication and the specific effect on teamwork.

Student attempt

Remote working reduced informal communication, which made it harder for teams to build trust and solve small problems quickly.

Toolkit follow-up

This is clearer because it names both the communication change and the effect. The next step is to support it: what evidence, example or source will show that informal communication matters for trust or problem-solving?

The important point is that the student makes the revision. The toolkit diagnoses, explains and asks for the next move.
