Student Help

Where does the toolkit fit into your study workflow?

Use the toolkit after you have made your own attempt, then turn feedback into learning, revision and honest AI-use records.

If the AI summarises the toolkit file instead of showing the menu, type: Do not summarise the file. Use it as instructions and show the menu.

How this toolkit helps

Think of the toolkit as a set of instructions that helps an AI behave more like a study tutor.

You still need to do the thinking, drafting and deciding. The toolkit helps the AI give you feedback, explain problems, ask useful questions and help you plan your revision.

If you get stuck

You can say “I’m stuck” at any stage. The tutor should take a step back and help you work out a manageable next move.

If it can tell why you are stuck, it should say what it thinks the problem is and offer help with that. If it is not clear, it should ask a short question rather than giving you a long list of tasks.

Core rule

Bring the toolkit something you have tried.

The toolkit works best when you have already made an attempt: a sentence, paragraph, section, plan, proposal, feedback note or rough draft. It can help you understand what is not working and decide what to do next.

What the toolkit is for

  • Getting feedback on your own writing.
  • Understanding repeated mistakes.
  • Checking paragraph and whole-draft structure.
  • Testing whether an argument is clear.
  • Turning feedback into a revision plan.
  • Practising a skill before revising your own work.

Readable feedback

The toolkit is designed to give short, readable guidance rather than endless bullet points. Some tools still use tables or lists when they help, but student-facing feedback should usually feel like a focused tutor conversation.

What it is not for

Do not use the toolkit to get an AI tool to write your assignment for you, invent evidence, disguise AI authorship, or bypass your course rules. You remain responsible for the final wording, claims, evidence and submission.

A simple student workflow

  1. Try first. Draft the sentence, paragraph, section or plan yourself.
  2. Choose one library. Use a mini library if you want focused help, or the master library if you need the full toolkit.
  3. Type prompt. Ask the AI to show the menu and choose the tool that fits your problem.
  4. Paste only what is needed. Use a short extract unless your course rules and privacy situation allow more.
  5. Revise yourself. Use the feedback to make your own changes rather than copying a finished version.
  6. Record AI use if needed. Use SW3 if your course asks for an AI-use statement or you want a transparent record.

Which tool should I start with?

Tool codes show which library a tool belongs to: WT = Writing Tutor, ST = Structure Tutor, AT = Academic Thinking, RP = Research Proposal, and SW = Study Workflow.

If your problem is…Try…
Grammar, wording or repeated mistakesWT3 — Find My Mistakes (Writing Tutor), then WT4 — Teach Me This Mistake (Writing Tutor)
A sentence sounds unclearWT1 — Clarity Clinic (Writing Tutor)
A paragraph does not workWT2 — Single Paragraph Analysis (Writing Tutor) or ST1 — Paragraph Structure Review (Structure Tutor)
Your style feels weak or informalWT5 — Style and Clarity Review (Writing Tutor)
Your argument is too descriptiveAT3 — Descriptive vs Analytical Check (Academic Thinking) or AT2 — Argument Map (Academic Thinking)
Your proposal feels vagueRP1 — Research Question, Aim and Objectives Checker (Research Proposal) or RP3 — Critical Research Supervisor Review (Research Proposal)
You have tutor feedback and feel stuckSW2 — Tutor Feedback to Action Plan (Study Workflow), then SW1 — Revision Plan (Study Workflow)

What not to paste or upload

Be careful with anything private or about other people. Do not paste names, student numbers, email addresses, interview transcripts, placement notes, client details, case studies, unpublished research, marks, feedback about another student, or confidential material unless you have checked your course, research ethics or institution rules.

What good toolkit feedback should feel like

Good feedback should be readable and manageable. It should help you understand one or two important problems, not bury you in a long list of things to fix.

Good feedback should protect your meaning. The toolkit should not make your work sound more academic by changing your ideas. If a suggested word may change the meaning, it should explain the difference and ask you to choose.

Good feedback should lead to your next move. The best response is not a finished answer. It should help you decide what to try next in your own writing.
